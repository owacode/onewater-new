What is One Water?

Our planet has a finite amount of water. And in today’s complex world, there are many competing demands for this precious resource. Water demands for consumption, industrial uses, agriculture, ecosystems and healthy habitat needs. And water demands and stresses are only getting more challenging as population grows and our climate is changing.

One Water is new way of thinking about how we value and manage water. One Water is based on the premise that all water has value. Not just potable water that we drink. But also wastewater that we can treat to serve non-potable demand or recharge groundwater aquifers. Rainwater and even urban stormwater can be recycled. The One Water framework looks at how to optimize the beneficial use of water in the entire water cycle. Considerations include recycling, capture, and storage. The end result is improved water quality, water resiliency and sustainability.

 One Water solutions can be implemented throughout the entire water cycle. It involves managing finite water resources for long-term resilience and reliability, meeting both community and ecosystem needs.

One Water is an integrated planning and implementation approach to a managing finite water resources for long-term resilience and reliability, meeting both community and ecosystem needs. Many innovative utilities and cities across the US have embraced the One Water approach by taking a more holistic look at the water challenges and need for institutional collaboration and stakeholder engagement to develop sustainable solutions. The One Water approach is inclusive and first of all promotes collaboration by between water, wastewater, recycled water, and stormwater management utilities. But it goes beyond that. By embracing other “non-water” agencies and stakeholders, creative One Water solutions can be implemented.

For example, collaboration with transportation agencies can pave the way for large scale green infrastructure along new roads and railways in a cost-effective manner. By working together early in the planning process, smart water management approaches can be incorporated in design and construction by agencies that typically do not consider water footprint or water quality improvements. For example, the Los Angeles Zoo incorporated green stormwater infrastructure and water recycling for many animal exhibits in the LA Zoo Master Plan as a result of collaboration with the One Water LA team.


One Water collaboration takes place at multiple levels, from executive management between various agencies to active engagement of stakeholders and the general public. (Photo: One Water LA stakeholder workshop #3, October 2016).

One Water is a movement that is sweeping across the nation. This paradigm shift incorporates sustainable and resilient water management strategies for utilities large and small that balances urban, rural, and environmental benefits. Together we can transform the way we manage our finite water resources as One Water.
